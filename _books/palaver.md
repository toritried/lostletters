Palaver is such an interesting word for a title because it can mean many things: idle talk / talking or discussion that goes on for too long and is not important, unnecessary work and trouble, a long discussion between persons of different cultures of levels of sophistication. I wonder how many of those meanings the author was trying to capture in this book.

This book focuses on the complicated relationship between a queer son, living in Tokyo, and his immigrant mother who surprises him with a visit from Houston, Texas. I resonated deeply with having a somewhat hidden life in Tokyo as well as a very fraught relationship with my mother, just like the main character. These parallels drew me in instantly. The two walk on eggshells around each other, choosing their words carefully, while an undercurrent of frustration pervades each conversation. The author gives words to an experience I know all too well:

<blockquote>
    She knew he was relying on a script. He'd say something inflammatory. She'd respond in-kind. The two of them would argue until they could no longer comfortably occupy the same space.
</blockquote>

Beyond that relationship, I think the author captures what it's like living in Tokyo as a foreigner well. I particularly resonated with this quote:
<blockquote>
    He knew that he enjoyed Japan's seasons. Enjoyed how his hyper-visibility folded into anonymity. Understood also that living in Tokyo, there was a ceiling to just how much he could accomplish. And, if he pushed, that ceiling simply wouldn't budge, but there was still more than enough room to get comfortable. 
</blockquote>

As the book went on, I started to get a little lost with how non-linear it was. This is a nit but the amount of English speakers for the mother to interact with in Tokyo was entirely unrealistic. I also didn't like the ending, where the wrong person apologized, so minus one star. 

Also, a bit of the Japanese neighborhood names were botched in the audiobook, so I probably should have read an ebook version myself to avoid that.
